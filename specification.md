# Specification

The lmer approach with respondent random intercepts is a valid and defensible alternative to the standard OLS + clustered SEs estimator for conjoint AMCEs. Causal identification comes from the randomisation of vignette components, not the estimator, so the choice of model does not threaten the AMCE interpretation. With random intercepts only (no random slopes), the conditional and marginal fixed effects coincide, meaning the lmer coefficients are population-average effects equivalent to OLS estimates. The efficiency advantage over clustered SEs is modest but real, and the Kenward-Rogers F-tests used for the interaction comparisons are the appropriate inferential tool in this mixed-model framework. One minor point: `re.form = NA` is conceptually cleaner than `re.form = NULL` in the `predictions()` calls for marginal means, as it gives population-level rather than BLUP-conditional predictions; in practice the difference is negligible for a balanced design but the former better reflects what AMCEs represent.

Respondent-level covariates (sex, education, parenthood status, etc.) are not needed for identification — randomisation and the random intercept already handle that — but their inclusion is justified on efficiency grounds. Covariates that predict baseline rating tendencies reduce residual variance and tighten standard errors on the vignette component estimates, following the logic of covariate adjustment in randomised experiments (Lin, 2013). They also ensure that the `KRmodcomp` hypothesis tests isolate the interaction terms cleanly by holding the control set constant across compared models. The main caution is researcher degrees of freedom: covariate selection should be justified on substantive grounds and ideally pre-specified, not chosen to optimise results.
